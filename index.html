BiomedParse Clinical Query

MEng capstone. A natural-language clinical-query interface over BiomedParse with a verified reasoning spine and abstention. External validation at Dice 0.965 on Shenzhen X-ray and CHAOS CT/MRI. Fertility-imaging fine-tuning in progress on Compute Canada.

PathoSearch

Open-source AI pathology retrieval over 9 organs (PLIP + KNN). Discovered a scanner-fingerprint bias in pathology embeddings and built a text-anchor fix that gave a 84% bias reduction, 97.7% cross-institution accuracy. Built for resource-limited clinics.

A model only learns to spot disease if you actually show it what disease looks like.

I didn't start out in AI. I came from mechanical engineering. But the first time I saw a defect-testing system catch a flaw the human eye would miss, I was hooked, so I started teaching myself image annotation in my own time.

That curiosity got me onto a medical R&D team working on retinal OCT scans of the macula. Looking at real datasets, a healthy eye sitting right next to a patient's, taught me the lesson I still carry around: the "defect" data isn't something to clean away, it's the whole point. A model can't tell sick from healthy unless you actually show it both.

My MEng has felt like one project building on the last. Digit recognition taught me how to build a full pipeline from scratch. Pose estimation pushed me to find where the standard methods fall apart. DoTA taught me to combine features, slices and tracking, which is exactly what 3D medical scans need. EndoSAM put it all together on real pelvic MRI. And right now my capstone is extending BiomedParse to the text side of ovarian imaging.

Honestly, every project has been the same cycle for me: find what's broken, figure out why, and fix it to get a better result. And every fix just makes me want to learn more. Because one day I really want to use these skills for something that genuinely helps people.
